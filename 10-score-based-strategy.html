Score-Based Strategy · the Match State Engine

Two identical balls demand different decisions at 30–0 and 0–30. The scoreboard changes the math of risk. This engine tells you how aggressive to be at every score.

The core principle

When you're ahead in a game, points are "cheaper" — you can take a calculated risk for a big reward. When you're behind, every point is expensive — play your highest-percentage tennis and make the opponent earn it.

🎯 The 0–30 example

At 0–30 the temptation is to "do something" — go big down-the-line. Wrong. The goal isn't a highlight, it's to reach 15–30 and rebuild the game. If it slips to 0–40 your hold probability falls off a cliff. So at 0–30: lower error probability, higher rally tolerance, trusted patterns. Level A

Tie-break

A tie-break is a pressure cauldron — so it's the wrong time for new ideas.

  • Aggression: medium — controlled, not reckless. Risk: low.
  • Serve: highest first-serve %, your most trusted locations.
  • Return: get it deep and in; make every serve be played.
  • Mental: mini-margins matter — win the first two points, take the early lead, breathe between points.
  • Avoid: untested patterns, the big bail-out shot, rushing.

⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Playing every point the same regardless of score.
  • Getting more aggressive when behind (raising error risk exactly when you can't afford it).
  • Getting passive when ahead (inviting the comeback) — see Closing-Out.
  • Trying something new on the biggest points instead of your Pressure-Point default.
🎾 Practice drill — "Start at the score"
  • Setup: play practice games that start at a called score (0–30, 40–0, deuce, ad-out).
  • Reps: rotate through 6 score states; state your aggression dial before each point.
  • Constraint: at "behind" scores you lose the point if you attempt a low-% direction change.
  • Coaching cue: "Read the score, set the dial, pick the pattern."
  • Success metric: correct aggression level chosen on ≥ 80% of points; hold % from 0–30 starts improving.
