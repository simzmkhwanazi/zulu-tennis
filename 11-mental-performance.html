Mental Performance

You play maybe 20% of a match; the other 80% is the time between points. Win that time with a repeatable routine and you'll execute your tactics when it matters most.

The between-point routine: Reset · Breathe · Analyze · Commit

A four-stage ritual that fits the ~20 seconds between points. Same sequence every time — it converts emotion into a plan and protects you from carrying the last point into the next.

1 · Reset (0–3 sec)

Physically release the last point: turn to the back fence, racquet into the non-dominant hand. Whatever happened — good or bad — it's over. No score talk in your head yet.

2 · Breathe (recover)

Walk to your towel/position with relaxed shoulders. Slow breaths to drop the heart rate. This is the physical reset that makes clear thinking possible.

3 · Analyze (plan)

Now think: read the score (set the aggression dial), recall the opponent's tendency, and choose one pattern and target. Decide before you step to the line.

4 · Commit (5 sec before)

Step in with a fixed pre-point ritual (bounce count, breath). Commit 100% to the chosen plan. Doubt is decided before the toss, not during the point.

Pressure situations — protocols

Momentum swings

Lost 4 games in a row? Slow everything down — use full time between points, towel off, reset the routine. Change something small (serve pattern, return position) to break the opponent's rhythm. Hold one service game to stop the bleeding; momentum is real but it turns on a single game.

Choking (tight on big points)

Choking = over-thinking outcomes. Antidote: shrink your focus to one external cue (watch the ball's seams, target the contact) and a trusted pattern — never a new one. Exhale on contact. Commit to the process, not the result. Level B

Tight matches (close sets)

Play the score, not the scoreboard pressure. Default to your Pressure-Point patterns, win the leverage points (30–30, deuce), and keep your routine identical to early in the match.

Crowd / external pressure

Narrow your world to your towel, your routine, and the ball. The crowd is noise, not information. The routine is your bubble.

Bad line calls

One protocol: state your case briefly if needed, then let it go in the Reset phase and re-commit. Carrying anger into the next point loses you a second point for free — that's the real cost of a bad call.

Frustration / anger

Have a physical release valve (a breath, a fist, a word) and a hard rule: it ends at the back fence. Channel it into intensity on the next first serve, not into reckless shot-making.

⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Rushing between points when nervous — speeds up exactly when you should slow down.
  • Analyzing before resetting — you plan while still angry, so the plan is emotional.
  • Changing tactics every point out of frustration with no commitment.
  • Treating the routine as optional on big points — that's when you need it most.
🎾 Practice drill — "Routine under load"
  • Setup: play practice points; you must perform the full 4-step routine on every point.
  • Reps: a partner/coach calls out a "distraction" (bad call, lost point) randomly; you run the routine anyway.
  • Constraint: skip a step → replay the point.
  • Coaching cue: "Reset, breathe, plan, commit — every single point."
  • Success metric: routine completed on ≥ 95% of points, including after errors.
✅ Success metrics
  • Fewer "double-fault the point after a bad one" sequences.
  • Win % on points after losing the previous point ↑.
  • Tie-break and deciding-set win % ↑ (track in Analytics).
