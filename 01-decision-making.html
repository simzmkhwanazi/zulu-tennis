The Decision Engine

The spine of the whole system. Tennis isn't won by nicer shots — it's won by better decisions, made automatically under pressure. This page turns "what should I hit?" into a repeatable, high-percentage process.

The only question that matters

Every shot decision answers one question:

🎯 The core question

Given the score, the situation, the opponent, the incoming ball and my balance — what is the highest-value decision that increases my probability of winning this point?

Not "what's the best shot in the world here," but "what's the best shot for me, right now, that I can repeat." Three priorities settle every close call:

Expected Value > highlight shots Repeatability > perfect execution Pressure reliability > practice level

The Tactical Decision Formula

Elite decisions feel instinctive because the inputs have been internalised. These are the ten the engine weighs — train them until the read is automatic:

⚖️ Inputs
  1. Score situation
  2. Point importance
  3. Court position
  4. Incoming ball quality
  5. Player balance
  6. Opponent position
  7. Opponent weaknesses
  8. Risk level
  9. Expected reward
  10. Recovery position after the shot
⚠️ Common mistake

Most players weigh only inputs 4 and 9 (the ball and the reward) and ignore 1, 5 and 10 — score, balance and recovery. That's how you hit a "great" shot at 0–30 and still lose the game.

The Ball-Quality Decision Engine

The universal entry point. Before patterns, before targets — classify the ball, then respond. Where the ball lands in your half sets the default:

🌳 Universal decision tree
INCOMING BALL
   │
   ▼
Is the ball ATTACKABLE?   (short · sitting up · inside the court)
   │
   ├─ NO  ─▶  Neutralise  ·  recover to home base  ·  extend the rally

   └─ YES ─▶  Can I attack IN BALANCE?
                 │

                 │
                 └─ YES ─▶  Attack  ▸  finish  ▸  recover

How patterns are written: the Decision Card

Flagship pattern: Home Base

Decision Card

Home Base Pattern

Level A
Match situation
Any neutral baseline rally — most often a crosscourt exchange — where you feel the urge to "do something" but don't yet have a ball worth attacking. Especially valuable on serve at 0–30 / 15–30.
Problem it solves
Stops the single most common unforced error in club tennis: changing direction off the wrong ball. It converts a vague "stay patient" into a concrete, automatic rule.
Decision rule
Picture an imaginary line running from you to your opponent. If the ball crosses that line (an "outside" ball) → do not change direction; return it back where it came from (crosscourt), with height and margin. If the ball does not cross the line (an "inside" ball) and you're balanced → direction change is unlocked: open court, inside-in, or inside-out.
Why it works
  • Geometry: redirecting an outside ball means hitting over the higher part of the net into less court — the lowest-percentage shot in tennis.
  • Probability: resetting crosscourt keeps the ball over the lowest net and into the longest diagonal — maximum margin.
  • Recovery: crosscourt-to-crosscourt keeps your recovery distance short; redirecting leaves the open court exposed.
  • Pressure on them: each crosscourt to a stretched opponent makes their next ball more likely to float back as an inside ball — which you then attack.
Wrong vs correct
✗ Wrong
0–30, stuck in a backhand crosscourt rally, you feel pressure and rip it down-the-line off a wide, outside ball. Low percentage → 0–40, likely the game.
✓ Correct
0–30, you reset the backhand crosscourt with margin, stretch them again, and attack the floaty inside ball that comes back. → 15–30, momentum shifts.
Decision tree
OPPONENT'S BALL
   │
   ▼
Did it cross your imaginary centre line?
   │
   ├─ YES (outside ball) ─▶ DON'T change direction
   │                        reset crosscourt — height + margin
   │                        recover ▸ wait for the inside ball
   │
   └─ NO  (inside ball) ──▶ Direction change UNLOCKED (if balanced)
                            ▸ open court  ▸ inside-in  ▸ inside-out
                            ▸ recover & keep the pressure on
See it — if the ball goes this way
Practice drill — "Cross until inside"
  • Setup: both players rally crosscourt only; either may change direction only when they receive an inside ball.
  • Reps: 6 × 4-minute games to 7; loser of each point feeds next.
  • Constraint: changing direction off an outside ball = lose the point automatically.
  • Coaching cue: "Cross, cross, cross… now." Say it out loud as you recognise the inside ball.
  • Success metric: < 2 illegal (outside-ball) direction changes per game.
Match application
Make this your default in every neutral rally and especially when behind on serve (0–30, 15–30, break point). When ahead (30–0, 40–0) you can widen the trigger and attack slightly earlier — see the Match State Engine.
Success metrics
  • Directional-change error rate ↓ (track in Match Analysis).
  • Average rally length ↑ on your service games.
  • Points won after resetting an outside ball ↑.
Opponent adaptations
  • vs a counter-puncher: add height/heaviness, not pace — outlast their patience for the inside ball.
  • vs a flat hitter: they'll feed you more inside balls; be ready to attack earlier.
  • vs a lefty: the "line" still applies — just remember the crosscourt diagonal now favours their forehand.
Skill-level adaptation
Intermediate: just obey the rule — never change direction off an outside ball. Advanced/Tournament: add disguise and vary height/spin on the reset to manufacture the inside ball sooner. See Skill-Level Roadmap.
