The only question that matters

Every shot decision answers one question:

🎯 The core question

Given the score, the situation, the opponent, the incoming ball and my balance — what is the highest-value decision that increases my probability of winning this point?

Not "what's the best shot in the world here," but "what's the best shot for me, right now, that I can repeat." Three priorities settle every close call:

Expected Value > highlight shots Repeatability > perfect execution Pressure reliability > practice level

The Tactical Decision Formula

Elite decisions feel instinctive because the inputs have been internalised. These are the ten the engine weighs — train them until the read is automatic:

⚖️ Inputs
  1. Score situation
  2. Point importance
  3. Court position
  4. Incoming ball quality
  5. Player balance
  6. Opponent position
  7. Opponent weaknesses
  8. Risk level
  9. Expected reward
  10. Recovery position after the shot
⚠️ Common mistake

Most players weigh only inputs 4 and 9 (the ball and the reward) and ignore 1, 5 and 10 — score, balance and recovery. That's how you hit a "great" shot at 0–30 and still lose the game.

The Ball-Quality Decision Engine

The universal entry point. Before patterns, before targets — classify the ball, then respond. Where the ball lands in your half sets the default:

Flagship pattern: Home Base

Decision Card

Home Base Pattern

Level A
Match situation
Any neutral baseline rally — most often a crosscourt exchange — where you feel the urge to "do something" but don't yet have a ball worth attacking. Especially valuable on serve at 0–30 / 15–30.
Problem it solves
Stops the single most common unforced error in club tennis: changing direction off the wrong ball. It converts a vague "stay patient" into a concrete, automatic rule.
Decision rule
Picture an imaginary line running from you to your opponent. If the ball crosses that line (an "outside" ball) → do not change direction; return it back where it came from (crosscourt), with height and margin. If the ball does not cross the line (an "inside" ball) and you're balanced → direction change is unlocked: open court, inside-in, or inside-out.
Why it works
  • Geometry: redirecting an outside ball means hitting over the higher part of the net into less court — the lowest-percentage shot in tennis.
  • Probability: resetting crosscourt keeps the ball over the lowest net and into the longest diagonal — maximum margin.
  • Recovery: crosscourt-to-crosscourt keeps your recovery distance short; redirecting leaves the open court exposed.
  • Pressure on them: each crosscourt to a stretched opponent makes their next ball more likely to float back as an inside ball — which you then attack.
Wrong vs correct
✗ Wrong
0–30, stuck in a backhand crosscourt rally, you feel pressure and rip it down-the-line off a wide, outside ball. Low percentage → 0–40, likely the game.
✓ Correct
0–30, you reset the backhand crosscourt with margin, stretch them again, and attack the floaty inside ball that comes back. → 15–30, momentum shifts.

Practice drill — "Cross until inside"
  • Setup: both players rally crosscourt only; either may change direction only when they receive an inside ball.
  • Reps: 6 × 4-minute games to 7; loser of each point feeds next.
  • Constraint: changing direction off an outside ball = lose the point automatically.
  • Coaching cue: "Cross, cross, cross… now." Say it out loud as you recognise the inside ball.
  • Success metric: < 2 illegal (outside-ball) direction changes per game.
Match application
Make this your default in every neutral rally and especially when behind on serve (0–30, 15–30, break point). When ahead (30–0, 40–0) you can widen the trigger and attack slightly earlier — see the Match State Engine.
Success metrics
  • Directional-change error rate ↓ (track in Match Analysis).
  • Average rally length ↑ on your service games.
  • Points won after resetting an outside ball ↑.
Opponent adaptations
  • vs a counter-puncher: add height/heaviness, not pace — outlast their patience for the inside ball.
  • vs a flat hitter: they'll feed you more inside balls; be ready to attack earlier.
  • vs a lefty: the "line" still applies — just remember the crosscourt diagonal now favours their forehand.
Skill-level adaptation
Intermediate: just obey the rule — never change direction off an outside ball. Advanced/Tournament: add disguise and vary height/spin on the reset to manufacture the inside ball sooner. See Skill-Level Roadmap.

02 Court Geometry
🎾 Courtside · Court Geometry A
Crosscourt = lower net, more court.
✓ Do — default crosscourt; aim 1–1.5 m over the net.
✗ Don't — aim the lines or skim the net.
Over the net, not at the lineRecover to the bisector
🎾 Drill: bisector recovery — hit deep CC, then beat the ball back to the bisector.

Why crosscourt is safer

Two facts of the court geometry make the crosscourt ball the highest-percentage shot in tennis — and they're not opinions, they're measurements:

Net at the centre
0.91 m
3′0″ · lowest point
Net at the posts
1.07 m
3′6″ · ~15 cm (6″) higher
Straight length
23.77 m
78 ft · baseline to baseline
Diagonal length
~25.2 m
82.5 ft · +1.4 m (4½ ft)

So the crosscourt ball travels over the lowest part of the net and into ~1.4 m (4½ ft) more court. Lower obstacle, bigger target — more margin for the same swing.

🎯 Tactical purpose

Default to crosscourt to bank margin, then change direction only when the ball quality (an inside ball) justifies the lower-percentage down-the-line. Level A

Margin over the net (aim up, not at the lines)

Amateurs aim at the lines and skim the net. Pros aim at a window above the net and let depth do the work. A ball aimed 1–1.5 m (3–5 ft) over the net still lands in — and almost never finds the net cord on a bad day. Depth, not flatness, is what pressures opponents.

⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Aiming for the lines: tiny target, huge downside (net or wide) for a marginal upside.
  • Flat, low clearance "to look aggressive" — it just raises your error rate.
  • Going down-the-line from a defensive, outside ball (over the highest net into the least court).

High-percentage targets

Think in zones, not dots. Your bread-and-butter targets are the deep crosscourt corner (maximum margin + pushes the opponent back and wide) and the deep middle (removes their angle, "safe" under pressure).

Percentage tennis (the 75/25 rule)

High-level singles is roughly ~75% primary patterns / ~25% secondary. The crosscourt rally is the primary; the down-the-line, drop, and approach are the 25% you deploy once the geometry is in your favour. The discipline isn't never going for it — it's only going for it on the right ball.

Recovery positions

Where you hit dictates where you stand next. Recover to the bisector of the opponent's likely replies — not always the centre mark. Hit crosscourt? Recover a step toward the side you hit to, because their best reply comes back crosscourt.

SituationRecovery position
Offensive (you're dictating)On or just inside the baseline, centred — ready to step in.
Defensive (stretched/behind)Deeper behind the baseline, central — buy time, cover the most court.
After a wide ballBisector of their angles — a step toward the side you hit to.
After an approachForward, split-step inside the service line, cover the line you approached down.
🌳 Recovery decision tree
YOU JUST HIT —
   │
   ▼
What did you hit?
   ├─ Deep crosscourt ─▶ recover just past centre, toward the side you hit
   ├─ Down the line ───▶ recover further across (you've opened your court)
   └─ Short / defensive ▶ recover deeper & central — buy time
🎾 Practice drill — "Bisector recovery"
  • Setup: coach/partner feeds you wide, alternating corners; you hit deep crosscourt and recover.
  • Reps: 4 × 90 seconds, 30s rest. Place a cone on the correct bisector spot each side.
  • Constraint: you must touch the cone (correct recovery) before the next feed.
  • Coaching cue: "Hit, then beat the ball back to the bisector."
  • Success metric: reach the recovery spot before the opponent's contact on ≥ 80% of balls.

Match application

Geometry runs quietly under every point: default crosscourt for margin, aim over a net window for depth, target zones (not lines), and recover to the bisector. It's the physics that makes the Home Base Pattern mathematically correct.

✅ Success metrics
  • Net errors ↓ as your average clearance rises (aim higher, land deeper).
  • Depth: a majority of rally balls landing in the back third of the court.
  • You're set and balanced for the next ball more often (recovery on time).

Serve +1 — winning the point on the first two balls

The serve isn't the end of the play — it's the set-up. Decide the +1 before you serve, based on the target. This is the cheapest source of points in tennis.

Decision Card

Wide Serve + 1 (Deuce court)

Level A
Match situation
Your service point, deuce court, you want a cheap, low-risk first-strike — especially 30–0, 40–0, or to start a game.
Decision rule
Serve wide to stretch the returner → take the +1 (usually a forehand) into the now-open court, away from the returner's recovery.
Why it works
The wide serve creates a geometric hole; the returner is recovering one way while you hit the other. You attack space, not the opponent.
Wrong vs correct
✗ Wrong
Big wide serve, then a +1 hit back behind the returner into the side they're already recovering to.
✓ Correct
Wide serve, +1 driven into the open court — or behind them only if you read them sprinting early.

Success metric
≥ 50% of wide-serve points end within the first 3 balls in your favour.
Opponent adaptation
vs a returner who blocks deep-middle, the court won't open — switch to body serves to jam them instead.

First-serve patterns

A first serve isn't "as hard as possible." It's location first, then pace. Build 2–3 go-to patterns per side and lean on them under pressure rather than improvising.

  • Deuce: Wide to open the court, or T for the cheap point.
  • Ad: T to the backhand (most players' weaker return), or wide to a one-handed backhand.
  • Mix in a body serve every few points so the returner can never commit early.

Second-serve patterns

The second serve defines your floor. The goal is not "safe and short" — that invites attack. It's a serve with spin, depth and a target that you can hit at 95%+ reliability under pressure.

🎯 Tactical purpose

A reliable, kicking second serve to the backhand removes the returner's free attack and keeps you in the point on equal terms. Level B

⚠️ Common mistakes
  • "Patty-cake" second serve landing short and central — the easiest ball in tennis to attack.
  • Aiming the second serve at the lines; double faults cost more than a slightly safer target.
  • No spin: a flat, slow second serve sits up perfectly for the returner.

  • Reps: 5 × 12 first serves calling the target before each; then 50 second serves to the backhand cone.
  • Constraint: second-serve block — you must make 9/10 to a deep target or restart the set.
  • Coaching cue: "Spin up and out, land it deep."
  • Success metric: first-serve location accuracy ≥ 50%; second-serve make rate ≥ 90% deep.

04 Return Strategy

✓ Do — 1st serve: block deep middle. 2nd serve: step in and attack.

The return's only job

The return doesn't have to be a weapon. Its job is to start the point on neutral or better terms and deny the server a free +1. The first measurable goal: get a high percentage of returns deep and in.

🎯 Tactical purpose

Take away the server's cheap point. A deep return through the middle removes the server's angle for their +1 and forces them to construct. Level A

First-serve vs second-serve returns

Two completely different mindsets. Don't use the same return goal for both.

Returning specific servers

vs a Big Server Level B

  • Stand back and shorten the swing — block, don't swing. Use their pace.
  • Target deep middle — biggest margin, kills their +1 angle.
  • Win the second-serve points ruthlessly; that's where big servers are beatable.
  • On big points, just make the return — force one more ball.

vs a Pusher / Junkballer

  • Their serve is soft — step in and take time, but don't over-hit the first ball.
  • Return deep and to a corner, then look to approach on the next short ball.
  • Patience: don't try to win the point on the return; win it by applying steady forward pressure.

vs a Left-hander

  • The lefty slice serve swings into the ad-court backhand — expect it on big points and shade that way.
  • Practise returning the wide ad serve; protect the backhand or run around to a forehand when you can.
  • Remember the diagonal: returning crosscourt now feeds their forehand — consider deep middle instead.
⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Same aggressive swing on first and second serves — frees up errors on the big first serve.
  • Standing in the same spot every time; vary depth and shade by score and server tendency.
  • Going for a return winner on break point instead of making the server play.
🎾 Practice drill — "Deep return target"
  • Setup: server serves first & second; place a deep-middle target zone (back third).
  • Reps: 6 games — return only; +1 point for any return landing in the back third.
  • Constraint: first-serve returns must be blocked (short backswing) to count.
  • Coaching cue: "Split-step on their toss; eyes on contact; deep and in."
  • Success metric: ≥ 70% of returns in play; ≥ 40% landing deep.

Match application

Pick a return position per serve type before the game starts, default to deep-middle on first serves, and pre-decide your second-serve attack. On the receiver's side, your "+1" is the return — the Decision Engine takes over from the third ball.

✅ Success metrics
  • Return-in-play rate ↑ (especially on first serves).
  • Return points won ↑, particularly off second serves.
  • Break points earned per return game ↑ over a season.

05 Baseline Patterns
🎾 Courtside · Baseline Patterns C
Crosscourt until the short ball.
✓ Do — lean on 2 primary patterns; play the weapon only on the right ball.
✗ Don't — run the finish (down-the-line) before you’ve earned it.
Earn it, then end it
🎾 Drill: "pattern on call" — coach calls A–F; you execute it.

Each pattern below is a play, not a single shot. The success % figures are rough planning heuristics (your mileage varies) to rank risk, not guarantees. Level C

Pattern A — Crosscourt until the short ball

The backbone. Rally crosscourt with margin and depth; the moment you get a short, inside ball, change direction and attack. This is the Home Base Pattern in action.

Use when: neutral, building, unsure. Risk: low · success ~75%.

Pattern B — Crosscourt, crosscourt, down-the-line

Two heavy crosscourts to move the opponent and earn an inside ball, then flip the third ball down-the-line into the space they've vacated.

Use when: opponent leans/cheats crosscourt. Risk: med · success ~60% (the DTL is the 25% shot — only on the right ball).

Pattern C — Heavy topspin, then open-court attack

Push the opponent back with high, heavy depth until they float a short reply, then step in and drive the open court.

Use when: opponent dislikes high balls / stands close. Risk: low · success ~70%.

Pattern D — Middle-ball pressure

Underrated. Deep, heavy balls down the middle remove the opponent's angles, jam their footwork, and induce errors — with very low risk to you.

Use when: under pressure, vs angle-hitters, big points. Risk: low · success ~78%.

Pattern E — Forehand dominance

Manufacture forehands. Run around the backhand to hit inside-out forehands, then finish inside-in into the open court — built around your biggest weapon.

Use when: you're a forehand-dominant player with court time. Risk: med · success ~62% (exposes your backhand corner — recover hard).

Pattern F — Backhand protection

If your backhand is the liability, protect it: use depth, slice and middle balls to avoid backhand-to- backhand exchanges you'll lose, and re-route to neutral.

Use when: your backhand is exposed or breaking down. Risk: low · success ~68%.

⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Running a pattern's finish (the DTL, the inside-in) before earning it — pattern collapses into a low-percentage gamble.
  • Having no pattern at all — reacting ball-to-ball, so the better-conditioned player wins.
  • Using Pattern E (forehand dominance) when you don't have the time/court position — leaving the backhand corner wide open.
🎾 Practice drill — "Pattern on call"
  • Setup: coach calls a pattern letter (A–F) at the start of each point; you must execute it.
  • Reps: 6 × 5-minute blocks, one pattern emphasised per block, then mixed.
  • Constraint: the "finish" shot only counts if it came off an inside/short ball.
  • Coaching cue: "Earn it, then end it."
  • Success metric: correct pattern executed on ≥ 70% of points; finish-error rate falling.

Match application

Pick your two primary patterns (usually A + D) and one weapon pattern (B, C or E) based on your archetype. Default to the primaries; deploy the weapon ~25% of the time on the right ball. Pre-load them as autopilot in the Pattern Library.

✅ Success metrics
  • You can name the pattern you're running mid-rally.
  • Finish-shot (DTL / inside-in) error rate stays below your crosscourt error rate.
  • Rally win % climbs as you stick to primaries under pressure.

🎾 Courtside · Attacking System A

✓ Do — approach down-the-line, then close the net.

Recognising the attackable ball

Three cues, ideally two or more present at once: the ball is short (landing inside the baseline), sitting up (above net height at contact), and you can take it in balance. Miss these and you're forcing, not attacking.

The "Should I attack?" checklist

📋 Attack only if…
  • The ball is landing inside the baseline (short).
  • You can contact it at or above net height (sitting up).
  • You are balanced and moving forward, not stretched.
  • You have a target already in mind (open court / behind them).

If two or more are missing → keep constructing. Don't gift the point.

🌳 Attack decision tree
SHORT BALL ARRIVES —
   │
   ▼
Can I take it in balance, at/above net height?
   ├─ NO ─▶ Rally ball deep, recover, stay patient
   └─ YES ─▶ Approach or drive?
              ├─ Very short / low ─▶ APPROACH + close net
              └─ Sitting up high ──▶ DRIVE / swing volley, finish

Transition footwork

The error most amateurs make: hitting the approach while still running, then stopping. Do the opposite — load, hit, then keep moving forward and split-step as the opponent strikes.

  • Read short early → explode forward.
  • Hit through the ball moving forward (don't camp behind it).
  • Continue toward the net; split-step just before their contact.
  • Close the line you hit down.

Approach-shot selection

The short ball is…Approach choiceWhy
Very low / awkwardSlice deep down-the-lineStays low, buys you time to close
Sitting up, mid-courtDrive deep down-the-lineShortest path to net to defend; pressures them
Wide & shortDrive behind them / sharp angleThey're recovering the other way
🎯 The golden rule

Approach down the line by default — it's the shortest distance to the net, so you have time to get into position and you only have to cover one passing lane. Level A

Closing & finishing

  • First volley: deep and simple — you don't have to win it outright, just stay on offense.
  • Position: close tight after a good approach; hang back a touch after a weak one.
  • Finish: into the open court or behind a scrambling opponent — see Net Play.
⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Attacking a ball that's deep or below net height — the #1 unforced-error source.
  • Approaching crosscourt (longer path to net, two passing lanes to cover).
  • Over-hitting the approach for a winner instead of setting up the volley.
  • Floating to the net without a split-step — you're a sitting duck for the pass.
🎾 Practice drill — "Short-ball green light"
  • Setup: partner feeds a mix of deep and short balls randomly.
  • Reps: 5 × 2-min. Attack only the genuine short balls; rally the deep ones.
  • Constraint: attacking a deep ball = lose the point; you must close the net after every approach.
  • Coaching cue: "Read it, step in, hit through, split-step."
  • Success metric: > 60% of approached points won; near-zero attacks on deep balls.
✅ Success metrics
  • Net points won % climbing (track in Analytics).
  • Approach-shot success rate > 60%.
  • Errors on "attacks" of deep/low balls trending to zero.

🎾 Courtside · Defensive System A

The goal of defense

When you're stretched, the worst instinct is to try to hit a winner from a losing position. Elite players do the opposite: they hit shots that buy time and reset the rally to even. One great defensive ball flips the pressure back onto the attacker.

🎯 Tactical purpose

Turn defense into neutral, neutral into offense. Every defensive shot answers: "what buys me the most time and the safest reset?" Level A

The four defensive tools

1 · High, deep defensive crosscourt

The #1 reset. High over the net (huge margin) + deep + crosscourt (longest court, shortest recovery). Buys the time you need to get back to home base.

2 · Defensive slice

When you can't get fully set, the slice keeps the ball low and deep, stays controlled off pace, and gives you extra time. Especially valuable on the backhand and on the dead run.

3 · Emergency lob

Pulled way out wide or rushed at net by the opponent? The high defensive lob resets everything — it buys maximum time and pushes them back off the net. Aim deep, over their backhand side.

4 · Reset to neutral

Not every ball under pressure must be spectacular. A simple deep ball down the middle removes the opponent's angle and lets you re-centre. Middle + deep = the safest reset there is.

Recovering from wide positions

After defending wide, don't sprint blindly to the centre mark — recover to the bisector of the angles your reply allows the opponent (see Court Geometry). A high, deep crosscourt buys enough time to make that recovery.

How much trouble are you in?
   ├─ Very late, on the run ─▶ SLICE deep / high CC — just survive
   ├─ Pulled wide, time-poor ▶ HIGH DEEP crosscourt, recover bisector
   ├─ Rushed at the net ─────▶ LOB deep over backhand
   └─ Mildly pressured ──────▶ RESET deep middle, re-centre
⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Going for a winner from a defensive position — low percentage, hands the point over.
  • Hitting flat and low under pressure (no margin) instead of high and deep.
  • Recovering to the centre mark by default instead of the bisector — leaving the open court exposed.
  • Panicking at the net rush instead of calmly lobbing.
🎾 Practice drill — "Survive & reset"
  • Setup: partner/coach hits you 4–6 aggressive balls corner to corner; your only job is to keep resetting.
  • Reps: 6 × 1-min scramble sets; count how many you reset deep (past the service line).
  • Constraint: every ball must clear the net by 1.2 m+ (4 ft+) — height = time.
  • Coaching cue: "High, deep, recover — don't be a hero."
  • Success metric: ≥ 70% of defensive balls land deep; you reach the bisector before their next contact.

Match application

The moment you read trouble, switch from "win" to "survive & reset." A patient defensive game frustrates attackers into errors and turns long matches in your favour — the core of the counter-puncher game.

✅ Success metrics
  • Points won from defensive positions ↑.
  • Reset-to-neutral rate after being pulled wide ↑.
  • Unforced errors while defending ↓ (height & depth doing the work).

08 Net Play System
🎾 Courtside · Net Play B
Volley deep. Then finish.
✓ Do — split-step on their contact; first volley deep, then close.
✗ Don't — try to kill the first volley or float in without a split-step.
Approach, split, deep, close
🎾 Drill: "approach, split, finish" — split-step every rep; first volley past the service line.

Positioning & anticipation

Win the net with your feet, not your hands. Close to the right depth, split-step on every contact, and stand on the bisector of the opponent's two passing lanes — biased toward the line you approached down.

The first volley

The first volley after an approach is a set-up, not a kill. Hit it deep and controlled, keep closing, and let the second volley finish. Trying to end it on the first volley is the biggest cause of net errors.

🎯 Tactical purpose

Stay on offense and force a passing attempt from a worse position. Deep first volley → close → finish. Level B

Reading the incoming ball

At the net the ball comes fast — its height and path decide the shot before you think. Gold = the incoming ball; ink = your reply.

Approach patterns & singles "poaching"

Singles has no partner, but the same principle applies: anticipate and cut off. When you read a predictable crosscourt reply, step across early and take it as a volley or swing volley. Approach down-the-line (shortest path), then poach the crosscourt pass you've invited.

⚠️ Common mistakes
  • No split-step — you're moving when they hit, so you can't change direction to the pass.
  • Trying to end the point on the first volley instead of building it.
  • Standing too far back (volleying up) or too close (lobbed easily) — find the right depth.
  • Overheads aimed at the lines; under pressure, deep-middle is the high-% overhead.
🎾 Practice drill — "Approach, split, finish"
  • Setup: coach feeds a short ball; you approach DTL, then they try to pass.
  • Reps: 5 × 10 approaches; alternate which pass they attempt; you must split-step every time.
  • Constraint: first volley must land past the service line (deep) to count.
  • Coaching cue: "Approach, split, deep volley, close, finish."
  • Success metric: ≥ 65% of net points won; split-step on 100% of contacts.

Match application

You don't have to live at the net — but you must be willing to finish there. Convert the short balls you earn in the Attacking System, and use the net to shorten points against grinders and on fast surfaces.

✅ Success metrics
  • Net points won % > 60% (track in Analytics).
  • First-volley depth: majority landing past the service line.
  • Overhead error rate ↓; pass-attempts-against converted into errors ↑.

Spotlight — Pressure Point

Decision Card

Pressure Point Pattern

Level A
Match situation
Break points, game points, 30–30, 4–4 — any point with outsized weight.
Decision rule
Go to your highest-percentage, most-practised pattern — never something new. Reduce variables: trusted serve location → trusted +1 → construct.
Why it works · risk · reward
Pressure shrinks skill; familiar patterns hold up best. Low risk, high reward — these points decide sets.
Common failures
Going bigger "because it's important," or getting passive and just pushing. Both raise error/again-attacked risk.
Practice progression
Play practice tiebreaks; start every server at 30–30; reward sticking to the trusted pattern, not winners.
Performance metric
Break-point save % and conversion %; deciding-point win %.

Spotlight — Closing Out

Decision Card

Closing-Out Pattern

Level B
Match situation
Serving for the set/match, or up a break and tightening.
Decision rule
Keep doing what built the lead — same intensity, same patterns. Raise first-serve % and rally margin slightly; do not get safe-passive or over-aggressive.
Why it works
Most blown leads come from changing a winning game. Consistency under "almost there" nerves is the skill.
Common failures
Tightening up and pushing (invites the comeback) or over-pressing for a quick finish.
Practice progression
Practice sets starting at 4–3, serving; or "serve it out" games from 40–30 up.
Performance metric
% of games closed when serving for set/match; leads converted.

  • Running high-risk patterns (Counterattack) on big points instead of Pressure-Point defaults.
  • Ignoring the matchup — match the pattern to the opponent (see Player Types).
