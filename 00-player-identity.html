Player Identity System

Before tactics, before drills — know who you are as a competitor. Your identity decides which patterns are yours, which to avoid, and where every training hour should go.

Why identity comes first

Most players borrow a game plan from whoever they last watched on TV. That's backwards. The highest-percentage strategy is the one you can execute repeatedly under pressure — which depends entirely on your strengths, your stamina, and your temperament. This page builds the profile that every later section plugs into.

🎯 Tactical purpose

Turn vague self-image ("I'm an attacking player") into a measured profile you can build a system around. Your archetype becomes the default lens for the Decision Engine, Pattern Library and Development Roadmap. Level B

The self-assessment Level C

Reading your scores → your archetype

Look at your highest three scores and your lowest two. The pattern points to an archetype. You won't be a pure type — you'll be a primary with a secondary. Build around the primary; protect the lows.

The 7 archetypes

Each archetype lists its signature, where it wins, where it bleeds, the core game plan, and what to train first.

1 · Aggressive Baseliner

Signature: dictates with heavy groundstrokes, takes time away, ends points from the back.

Wins on: first-strike tennis, forehand+1 patterns, short balls. Bleeds on: unforced errors when rushing, long defensive rallies, low-percentage windows.

Game plan: serve+1 to the forehand, hunt the short ball, attack only the inside ball. Train first: serve placement, forehand recovery footwork, error discipline.

2 · Counter-Puncher

Signature: elite defense and consistency; absorbs pace and redirects; wins by attrition.

Wins on: long rallies, opponent impatience, retrieving. Bleeds on: being rushed, short balls they must finish, opponents who shorten points.

Game plan: depth + height for margin, extend rallies, counter off the stretch. Train first: endurance, passing shots, transition finishing.

3 · All-Court Player

Signature: comfortable everywhere; selects the shot that exposes the opponent.

Wins on: variety, problem-solving, neutralizing one-dimensional opponents. Bleeds on: indecision, "jack-of-all-trades" with no clear weapon.

Game plan: read the matchup, deploy the right pattern, approach behind the short ball. Train first: decision speed, one signature weapon, net conversion.

4 · Serve-Dominant Player

Signature: holds easily off a big serve; cheap points; serve+1 dependence.

Wins on: hold games, tiebreaks, pressure-free service points. Bleeds on: return games, second-serve days, long baseline exchanges.

Game plan: protect serve, raise return aggression in spots, shorten points. Train first: return depth, baseline consistency, second-serve reliability.

5 · Grinder

Signature: physical, relentless, high rally tolerance; makes you earn every point.

Wins on: fitness, heat, opponent frustration. Bleeds on: being out-paced early, having to create offense, short windows.

Game plan: heavy depth, change of direction only on the right ball, drag opponents into long matches. Train first: offense from neutral, finishing, serve weapons.

6 · Net Attacker

Signature: seeks the net, finishes with volleys/overheads, applies positional pressure.

Wins on: forward pressure, fast courts, opponents who dislike pace. Bleeds on: passing shots, lobs, slow high-bouncing courts.

Game plan: approach behind deep balls, split-step early, close tight. Train first: first volley, overhead, approach-shot depth.

7 · Hybrid

Signature: blends two styles by score/opponent (e.g. counter-punch when behind, attack when ahead).

Wins on: adaptability, unpredictability. Bleeds on: losing the thread of which identity to use under pressure.

Game plan: pre-decide triggers (score/opponent) for each mode. Train first: the Match State Engine so mode-switching is automatic, not emotional.

⚠️ Common mistakes
  • Choosing an aspirational identity (the one you wish you had) over your real, repeatable one.
  • Trying to fix your weakest shot into a weapon instead of protecting it and amplifying a strength.
  • Switching identity mid-match out of frustration with no trigger or plan.
